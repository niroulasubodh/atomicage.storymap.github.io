üìç Hiroshima, Japan

Suzuki Akari

Atomic Bomb Survivor, Age 12 in 1945

Suzuki was walking to school on August 6, 1945, when the atomic bomb exploded. She survived with severe burns and scars. People treated her badly because they feared radiation. She had nightmares for years and struggled to live a normal life.

"The Atomic Cafe" shows happy government films about nuclear power, but hides the real suffering of survivors like Suzuki.
Radiation Sickness Social Stigma Lifelong Trauma
üìç Marshall Islands, Pacific Ocean

Alson Kabua

Island Resident Affected by Nuclear Testing

The U.S. tested 67 nuclear bombs in the Marshall Islands. Alson was seven years old in 1954 when radioactive fallout fell like snow. Children played in it without knowing it was deadly. He got sick immediately. His skin blistered and hair fell out. Many people got cancer. His island home became poisoned and his family had to leave forever.

"The Atomic Cafe" shows how the U.S. government told people nuclear bombs were safe. Pacific Islanders like Alson paid the real price.
Environmental Destruction Forced Relocation Generational Cancer
üìç Nevada Test Site, USA

Jane Watson

"Downwinder" Lived near nuclear test sites

Jane grew up in Salt Lake City in the 1950s when nuclear tests were common. The government said the tests were safe and even encouraged families to watch the mushroom clouds. She remembers having picnics to view the explosions. By age 24, she had thyroid cancer. Her mother died of cancer. Many of her neighbors died of cancer too. The government had lied to them.

"The Atomic Cafe" shows propaganda films telling Americans that nuclear weapons kept them safe. Jane's story shows the truth: many Americans got sick from their own government's bombs.
Government Betrayal Cancer Clusters Lost Trust
üìç Nagasaki, Japan

Kenji Watanabe

Postal Worker and Bomb Survivor

Kenji was delivering mail on August 9, 1945, when the atomic bomb exploded over Nagasaki. The heat burned away the flesh on his back and arms. He spent almost two years in the hospital. He survived but lived with constant pain. Later, he became an activist and showed photos of his injuries to world leaders at the United Nations to warn them about nuclear weapons.

"The Atomic Cafe" shows cheerful atomic propaganda. Kenji showed the world the real images, the true horror of nuclear war that governments tried to hide.
Lifelong Pain Activism Born from Trauma Visual Testament
